\section{Introduction}

The Gap A answer-pair generator problem asks for a native generator of the reciprocal WXYZTI answer-pair universe.  Project 21 established a bounded sequence of finite checkpoints.  The reciprocal pair grammar is exact over the realized row-pair universe, but Gap A remains open because the candidate universe has not yet been generated from native provenance alone.

A later checkpoint in Project 21 reduced the shared\_B selection problem to a 16-candidate universe.  Inside that universe, the selector is exact.  The selected cells are candidates
\[
    [1,6,8,15],
\]
with zero false positives and zero misses.

The visible selection matrix has a striking form.  The selected mapping from C-cycle rows to anchor-cycle columns is
\[
    [1,2,0,3],
\]
with cycle structure \((0\ 1\ 2)(3)\).  This looks like a twist.  However, after lifting both sides into native shell/rank coordinates, the same selector becomes identity.

This paper names and studies that mechanism:

\[
    \textbf{Lift \& Twist}.
\]

The working idea is simple.  Lift exposes the hidden shell/rank coordinate.  Twist is the coordinate cost of returning that hidden identity to the visible register order.
